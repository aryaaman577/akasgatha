# Seasons - Scientific Explanation

Seasons result from Earth's axial tilt, not distance from the Sun. Earth's axis tilts approximately 23.5 degrees relative to its orbital plane.

## Axial Tilt Creates Seasons

As Earth orbits the Sun, this tilt causes different hemispheres to receive varying amounts of direct sunlight throughout the year.

**Summer**: When a hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, sunlight strikes more directly and daylight hours are longer, producing warmer temperatures.

**Winter**: When a hemisphere tilts away from the Sun, sunlight strikes at a lower angle and daylight hours are shorter, producing colder temperatures.

**Spring and Autumn**: Transitional seasons when neither hemisphere tilts significantly toward or away from the Sun.

## Distance Misconception

Earth is actually slightly closer to the Sun during Northern Hemisphere winter. Distance has minimal effect compared to axial tilt. If distance mattered most, both hemispheres would experience summer or winter simultaneously.

## Equinoxes and Solstices

**Equinoxes** (around March 20 and September 22): Sun is directly over the equator; day and night are nearly equal length worldwide.

**Solstices** (around June 21 and December 21): Maximum tilt toward or away from the Sun; longest or shortest day of the year.

## Opposite Seasons

When it's summer in the Northern Hemisphere, it's winter in the Southern Hemisphere, and vice versa.
