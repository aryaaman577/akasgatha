# Satellite Orbits - Basic Principles

Artificial satellites orbit Earth following the same gravitational principles that govern planetary motion. They remain in orbit by balancing gravity's pull with forward momentum.

## How Satellites Stay in Orbit

A satellite must travel fast enough horizontally that as gravity pulls it downward, the curve of its fall matches Earth's curvature. It continuously "falls" around Earth rather than crashing into it.

## Orbital Velocity

The speed needed to maintain orbit depends on altitude. Lower orbits require faster speeds:
- Low Earth Orbit (LEO, ~400 km): ~28,000 km/h
- Geostationary Orbit (~36,000 km): ~11,000 km/h

## Types of Orbits

**Low Earth Orbit (LEO)**: 200-2,000 km altitude. Used for Earth observation and the International Space Station.

**Geostationary Orbit**: About 36,000 km altitude. Satellites match Earth's rotation, appearing stationary above one location. Used for weather and communication satellites.

**Polar Orbit**: Passes over both poles, allowing coverage of entire Earth as planet rotates beneath.

## Why Satellites Don't Fall

Satellites do experience drag from the thin upper atmosphere, which gradually slows them. LEO satellites eventually fall unless periodically boosted back to proper altitude.

## Launching Satellites

Rockets carry satellites above the atmosphere and accelerate them to orbital velocity. The direction and speed determine the satellite's orbital characteristics.
