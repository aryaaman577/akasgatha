# Planetary Motion - Basic Principles

Planets move in elliptical orbits around the Sun, held in place by gravitational force. This motion follows predictable mathematical laws.

## Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

**First Law (Law of Ellipses)**: Planets orbit the Sun in elliptical paths with the Sun at one focus.

**Second Law (Law of Equal Areas)**: A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This means planets move faster when closer to the Sun.

**Third Law (Harmonic Law)**: The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its average distance from the Sun.

## Why Planets Stay in Orbit

Gravity pulls planets toward the Sun, but their forward motion prevents them from falling into it. The balance between gravitational pull and forward momentum creates stable orbits.

## Orbital Periods

Planets closer to the Sun orbit faster. Mercury completes an orbit in 88 Earth days, while Neptune takes about 165 Earth years.

## Retrograde Motion

From Earth's perspective, outer planets sometimes appear to move backwards against the background stars. This is an illusion caused by Earth overtaking the slower-moving outer planet in its orbit.
