# Black Holes - Basic Explanation

A black hole is a region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from inside a boundary called the event horizon.

## Formation

Most black holes form when massive stars (at least 20-25 times the Sun's mass) exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse under their own gravity. The collapse compresses the star's mass into an extremely small volume.

## Event Horizon

The event horizon is the boundary around a black hole beyond which nothing can return. It's not a physical surface but rather the point of no return determined by the black hole's gravity.

## Types of Black Holes

**Stellar Black Holes**: Formed from collapsed massive stars, typically 5-100 solar masses.

**Supermassive Black Holes**: Found at galaxy centers, containing millions to billions of solar masses.

**Intermediate and Primordial Black Holes**: Theoretical types still being studied.

## Detection

Black holes cannot be seen directly, but astronomers detect them through:
- Effects on nearby matter and stars
- X-rays from heated material spiraling into the black hole
- Gravitational effects on light (gravitational lensing)
- Gravitational waves from merging black holes

## Common Misconception

Black holes don't "suck" everything nearby. Their gravitational pull follows the same inverse-square law as any other mass. If the Sun were replaced by a black hole of equal mass, Earth's orbit would remain unchanged.
