# Telescopes - How They Work

Telescopes collect and focus electromagnetic radiation (usually light) to create magnified images of distant objects. They extend human vision far beyond what the naked eye can observe.

## Basic Principles

Telescopes work by gathering more light than the human eye can collect. Larger apertures (openings) collect more light, revealing fainter objects and finer details.

## Main Types

**Refracting Telescopes**: Use lenses to bend (refract) light to a focus. The Galilean telescope was a refractor.

**Reflecting Telescopes**: Use curved mirrors to gather and focus light. Most large modern telescopes are reflectors because mirrors can be made larger and lighter than lenses.

**Catadioptric Telescopes**: Combine lenses and mirrors for compact, versatile designs.

## Beyond Visible Light

Modern telescopes observe across the electromagnetic spectrum:
- Radio telescopes detect radio waves from space
- Infrared telescopes see heat signatures
- X-ray and gamma-ray telescopes observe high-energy phenomena

## Space-Based vs Ground-Based

Space telescopes like Hubble and James Webb avoid atmospheric distortion and can observe wavelengths blocked by Earth's atmosphere. Ground-based telescopes are larger and easier to maintain but must compensate for atmospheric effects.

## Resolution and Magnification

A telescope's power isn't just magnification—resolution (the ability to distinguish fine details) depends primarily on aperture size and observing conditions.
